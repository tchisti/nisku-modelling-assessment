How to use this guide

You are looking at a Nisku Formation CO₂ storage feasibility study. The data comes from dozens of files, three decades of regional studies, and a lot of jargon. This guide is the Rosetta Stone.
Plain English

We are trying to answer one question: Can the Nisku limestone under central Alberta safely store 25 million tonnes of CO₂ over 25 years, without blowing anything up, leaking anywhere, or stepping on a neighbour's well?

To answer that we need to know four things:

  1. Will the CO₂ fit? → Capacity analysis
  2. Can we push it in fast enough? → Injectivity analysis
  3. Where does it go once it's down there? → Plume migration analysis
  4. How far does the pressure kick travel? → Area of Review (AOR) analysis

Every tab in the Excel workbook and every section in the HTML model is one puzzle piece that helps answer those four questions.

Technical

The Nisku Formation is an Upper Devonian carbonate ramp. The Pine/Oak/Cedar injection zone is ~65 m gross at 1010–1090 m TVDSS (mid-depth 1050 m); petrophysics is anchored on two deeper cored analog wells in the same formation (15-32-050-03W5 Enbridge, Nisku at 1863–1920 m; 8-17-051-03W5 TransAlta) that provide direct rock-property control. The ~800 m gap between the analog cores and the project injection depth is itself a structural assumption that needs a depth-structure map to defend. Regional context comes from Stoakes (2010), Machel (2005), and the WASP multi-operator study. We apply CSA Z741 / Bachu (2008) volumetric capacity; Mathias (2009) radial injectivity; Nordbotten-Celia (2006) plume similarity; Theis (1935) pressure diffusion + EPA Eq. 5-1 USDW-lift for AOR; Kaldi-Bachu (2009) site screening criteria.

The 4-question CCS workflow

All of reservoir characterization for CCS ladders into four questions. Every tab serves one of them.
QuestionPlain EnglishPhysicsTabs that answer it
Capacity
Will it fit?		 Is there enough empty rock pore space to hold 25 Mt of CO₂? Static volumetrics. Multiply rock volume × porosity × density × efficiency. Core_15_32, Core_08_17, Core_Stats, Fluid_Properties, Capacity, MC_Capacity
Injectivity
Will it flow in?		 Can we push 1 Mt/yr through a single well without cracking the rock? Radial flow equation with fracture-pressure safety limit. Inputs, Injectivity, DFIT, SCAL
Plume
Where does it go?		 After 25 years, how big a circle of CO₂ sits in the reservoir? Volumetric balance plus buoyancy/mobility correction. Plume, Fluid_Properties, SCAL
AOR
How far does the pressure reach?		 Pressure travels further than CO₂. Which existing wells feel enough pressure to leak? Transient pressure diffusion (Theis well function). AOR_Theis, Legacy_Wells

Containment — the sanity-check overlay

On top of those four, there is a containment question: "If we put CO₂ in, will the caprock hold it?" — that's what MICP, XRD, and the DFIT sheets answer. Those tabs don't drive the commercial case directly, but a failure on any of them kills the project instantly.

Key framing. The four questions are not equally binding. For Wabamun Nisku, the tightest constraint is AOR pressure-front radius, not volume capacity or injectivity. Pressure reaches further than the plume, and that's what sets the legacy-well integrity workload.

Where the assumed values came from

Every input in the model ladders to one of three categories:

Plain English

Measured from our cores — porosity, permeability, grain density. These come straight from the 45-sample 15-32 dataset. Most reliable.

From regional data — pressure gradient, fracture gradient, fluid properties. These come from the basin — good enough for Phase 1, should be confirmed on-site.

Literature defaults — efficiency factor, relative permeability exponents, seal entry pressure. Assumed from published analogs. Biggest source of uncertainty.

Technical — provenance

Direct measurement: φ, Kmax, Kv, ρgrain, h_gross → Schlumberger Carbon Services file 52131-2023-01375.

Regional inference: p_gradient (7.5 kPa/m disposal, 10.5 kPa/m virgin hydrostatic), fg (16.5 kPa/m), T_gradient (30 °C/km), salinity (210,000 mg/L).

Literature defaults: E (Bachu 2015), krg* (Corey/Brooks-Corey 0.45), Swi (SCAL 0.22), Pentry (Calmar shale analog 12.5 MPa lab Hg/air → 0.68 MPa CO₂/brine in-situ).

Regional & analog studies

These five studies are why we believe the Nisku can do this job. Each one gives us a reference point to calibrate against.

WASP — The Wabamun Area Sequestration Project

Plain English A multi-operator study (~2010-2012) that assessed the Nisku across the Wabamun area for CCS. It's the regional grandfather: it set expectations that the Nisku can hold ~100 Mt per township (93 km²). Our P50 at 500 km² × 3% efficiency → ~24 Mt, which scales to ~150 Mt/township. On the optimistic side of WASP, but within range.
Technical Bachu et al. The_Wabamun_Area_Sequestration_Project_WASP. Multi-model integration, static + dynamic simulations. Used as P50 anchor for lease-level capacity. WASP regional P50 efficiency converged at 3–4% after pressure-management realization.

ERCB-AGS Special Report 095 — Brazeau Nisku Q Pool

Plain English An actual Nisku reservoir that has been producing gas for decades. We get 40 years of pressure-production history — it's a real-world calibration point that proves the Nisku holds pressure, and proves what permeability looks like at scale.
Technical AER Special Report. Pool-level Kh from history-match ~20-50 D·m. Decline analysis gives aquifer strength. Confirms regional compartmentalization scale.

Machel 2005 — Nisku Acid Gas Injection

Plain English People have been injecting sour gas (H₂S + CO₂) into the Nisku for 20+ years at existing disposal wells. It works. This is the closest real-world precedent to what we're proposing.
Technical Acid gas injection (AGI) — typically 1–3% of full CCS rates, at existing infrastructure. Performance data informs skin estimates, halite-plugging occurrence, near-well geochemistry.

Stoakes 2010 — Regional Nisku Study

Plain English A full geological picture of the Nisku in this area: where it is, how thick it gets, what facies exist (reef buildups vs open shelf vs basin), how the rock changes with diagenesis. Six analog cored wells. Isopach maps. This is our regional context.
Technical Defines three depositional settings: Reef (Kmax 500-5000 mD), Open Marine (50-500 mD, our 15-32 fit), Off-Reef Basin (tight). Isopach shows 30-70 m thickness across Wabamun. Cross-sections A-A' through F-F' orient our lease within the regional trend.

CDL 2023 — Enbridge Wabamun Hydrogeological Study

Plain English The freshest look at the regional Nisku water system, commissioned specifically for the Enbridge Wabamun project. Tells us how hydraulically connected the aquifer is, where the brine flows naturally, and what's in the water chemically.
Technical CDL EBWH FINAL Report. Regional pressure gradient 7.5 kPa/m (underpressured vs virgin 10.5). TDS ~210,000 mg/L Na-Ca-Cl brine. Mild to moderate compartmentalization on township-scale. Nisku connected to underlying Wabamun through fractured contacts locally.

Wabamun Deep Nisku (1250 m) — AER D065 + Class VI Framework

A parallel deep-Nisku scenario at ~1250 m TVDSS in the Wabamun area, built to AER Directive 065 and US EPA UIC Class VI standards. Lives alongside the shallow Pine/Oak/Cedar case so depth-driven differences in pressure, CO₂ density, frac margin, and PISC duration are explicit.

Why a deeper scenario

Plain English Going from 1050 m to 1250 m adds ~200 m of overburden, which means: (a) more pressure (12.3 MPa vs ~10 MPa) so CO₂ is denser and stores more efficiently per pore volume, (b) more frac margin (BHP cap 22.5 MPa vs ~17 MPa) so we can inject faster, (c) more heat (55 °C vs ~50 °C) which slightly reduces CO₂ density. Net effect — deeper is usually better for storage efficiency, but the regulatory bar (Class VI) is higher because the AOR can extend further.
Technical Hydrostatic P_init = 9.81 × 1250 = 12 263 kPa. T_init = 17.5 + 0.030 × 1250 = 55 °C. NIST-corrected ρ_CO₂ at P,T = 615 kg/m³ (vs 710 at shallow). Frac P = 20 × 1250 = 25 000 kPa; BHP cap = 22 500 kPa (0.9 × frac). MSP gate (σ_h,min × depth) = 18 × 1250 = 22 500 kPa — BHP cap sits right at MSP, so geomech becomes the binding constraint, not frac. Adjust σ_h,min gradient (or BHP cap) in the deck builder if you have site-specific stress data.

11 regulatory fixes applied to the FABLE-5 spec

Technical · AER D065 + Class VI The CLAUDE FABLE-5 simulation prompt was reviewed against AER Directive 065 and 40 CFR §146.81-95 (UIC Class VI). Eleven items were corrected before adoption as the default in the deck builder:
  1. Brine ρ_SC = 1100 kg/m³ (was 1020) — consistent with 150 000 ppm TDS.
  2. Calmar caprock gridded (2 layers, K = 0.001 mD, Pe = 300 kPa) — required for D065 containment demonstration.
  3. Ireton underburden gridded (1 layer) — required for downward-migration accounting.
  4. PISC = 50 yr (was 5) — Class VI default; AER plume-stabilization expectation.
  5. Carter-Tracy analytical aquifer (PV-multiplier kept as fallback) — Class VI-defensible boundary.
  6. Sgr = 0.20 (was 0.12) — within published Wabamun-area carbonate range; raises residual-trapping benefit.
  7. Pe = 75 kPa (was 20) — typical for dolomitic carbonate at φ = 7.6 %.
  8. 3-component fluid CO₂-H₂O-NaCl (was 2) — captures salting-out at 150 000 ppm.
  9. ρ_CO₂ = 615 kg/m³ (was 640) — NIST value at 12.26 MPa / 55 °C.
  10. Static MSP gate added (σ_h,min × depth) — Directive 065 fracture-propagation gate.
  11. Trapping mass partitioning reported (mobile / residual / dissolved / mineral) — required for AER MMV annual reports.

Analytical proxies used in the deck builder visualizations

Technical · references
  • Nordbotten, Celia & Bachu (2005) — sharp-interface radial plume in homogeneous aquifer. r²(t) = M(t)/(ρ_CO₂ · π · H · φ · (1 – S_wi – S_gr)).
  • Nordbotten & Celia (2006) — gravity-override tongue, self-similar dimensionless interface.
  • Theis (1935) — radial pressure diffusion. ΔP(r,t) = (Q·μ)/(4πkH) · W(u). Cody-Thacher rational approximation for E₁(u). Post-injection ΔP via superposition (image well at –t_inj).
  • Spycher & Pruess (2005) + Li & Nghiem (1986) — CO₂ solubility in brine with salting-out correction; default ultimate dissolved fraction 30 % at 12.26 MPa / 55 °C / 150 000 ppm.
  • 40 CFR §146.81–95 (UIC Class VI) + AER Directive 065 Appendix P (MMV) — set the PISC duration, the AOR threshold (ΔP ≥ 10 kPa here), and the trapping-mechanism reporting requirement.

These proxies are for screening only. The .dat the deck builder emits is intended to be loaded into CMG-GEM (or equivalent compositional simulator with full PR/Li-Nghiem EOS and Carter-Tracy aquifer) for the regulatory submission. The browser visualization confirms the deck is internally consistent and produces physically plausible plume extents and AOR before you commit to the full run.

Tab 4 · Core_08_17

Plain English Same kind of data as 15-32, but from a different well (TransAlta 8-17, ~11 km away). The rocks look similar on a map, but the properties are dramatically worse. Why? Same formation, different diagenetic history — 8-17 got cemented and tightened over geologic time. This tab exists to prove that point.
Technical Schlumberger JIP CL-54283 (Jan 2012). 20 FD samples, Nisku 1794.7–1854.6 m. φ p50 = 5.1%, Kmax geomean = 6.8 mD, Kv/Kmax = 0.23. Different Kv/Kmax ratio tells us 8-17 is less layered — either more burrowed/homogenized, or the primary layering was obliterated by cementation.

Tab 6 · Fluid_Properties

Plain English What the CO₂ and the brine look like down there. At ~1050 m and ~36.5 °C, CO₂ is only just supercritical — and only if the reservoir is near brine-hydrostatic pressure. If the measured subhydrostatic regime holds, CO₂ is near-critical and far less dense (gas-like). The brine is heavy salty water. The density ratio drives how fast and how far CO₂ spreads.
Technical Brine-hydrostatic: P_init = 11.3 MPa, T = 36.5 °C → supercritical CO₂, ρ_CO₂ ≈ 715 kg/m³ (PR+Péneloux, ≈ Span-Wagner). μ_CO₂ ≈ 0.05 cP. Subhydrostatic (measured 7.58 kPa/m): P_init ≈ 8.0 MPa → ρ_CO₂ ≈ 340 kg/m³ (near-critical). Brine: ρ_w = 1,100 kg/m³, μ_w = 0.6 cP. Mobility ratio M = (μ_w · krw*)/(μ_CO₂ · krg*) ≈ 9. Δρ ≈ 385 kg/m³ (brine-hydro) drives buoyancy. Plume footprint now uses an explicit gravity-override sweep efficiency (E_sweep ≈ 0.35), not a ×√M radius multiplier.
Why mobility matters. M>1 means CO₂ fingers through the brine rather than pushing it out like a piston. Our M = 8 is an "unfavourable" displacement — typical for CCS. The plume doesn't move as a neat circle; the leading edge reaches ~2.8× further than volume-balance would predict.

Answer at defaults: ~24 Mt. Target: 25 Mt. We're on the edge — which is why pressure management (bumping efficiency from 3% to 4%) or larger area matters so much.

Tab 8 · MC_Capacity (Monte-Carlo)

Plain English Instead of calculating capacity once with "best guesses", we run it 10,000 times, each time pulling slightly different values from the plausible range of inputs (area, porosity, efficiency, etc.). The result is a distribution — 10% chance it's at least X, 50% chance at least Y, 90% chance at least Z. This is how uncertainty gets quantified.
Technical 10,000 independent samples. Distributions: Triangular for area / h / NTG / Swi / ρ_CO₂ / E; Normal for φ (p10-p50-p90 from 45 samples). No correlation applied in the sheet (simplification — the HTML model optionally applies φ↔K Pearson 0.7).

Headline output

  • Area and E dominate the spread → variance-reduction priority is a well-designed pressure-management strategy

Tab 9 · Injectivity

Plain English Can a single well take 1 Mt/yr without cracking the rock? Answer: yes — with ~7 MPa of headroom. We calculate the bottomhole pressure needed to push that rate through the formation, and check it against 90% of the minimum horizontal stress (the AER safety ceiling). ΔP required = 1.0 MPa; ceiling is 7 MPa above initial pressure. You could probably inject 3–5 Mt/yr per well before hitting the limit.
ΔPbh = (Qres · μCO₂) / (2π · k · krg · hperf) × [ln(re/rw) + s]

What the "Rate sensitivity" sub-table shows

Scans Q from 0.5 to 3.0 Mt/yr, reports BHP and OK/EXCEEDED status at each. The break-point is around 3.5 Mt/yr under base inputs.

Tab 10 · Plume

Plain English Where does the CO₂ actually go? We calculate the radius of the plume circle at 1, 2, 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 50, 100 years. At 25 years, radius ≈ 6.2 km. That's the "mass" of CO₂ under the surface — not where the pressure reaches. Plume is smaller than pressure footprint (which is the AOR).
r(t) = √[V(t) / (π · h · φ · (1−Swi))] × √M
Technical V(t) = Qres · t (reservoir-volume throughput). Then divide by accessible pore volume per unit area (h · φ · (1-Swi)) to get footprint area, then radius. The √M factor corrects for the fact that CO₂ is more mobile than brine — it fingers forward rather than displacing as a piston.

Tab 11 · AOR_Theis

Plain English The most important regulatory tab. Pressure spreads through the aquifer much further and faster than the CO₂ plume. AOR = "Area of Review" = the radius where the pressure increase is big enough to push brine up an old leaky well toward drinking water. The threshold is not a fixed 1.5 MPa — it is computed from reservoir/USDW geometry via EPA Eq. 5-1 (USDW-lift). At 1050 m under brine-hydrostatic pressure the reservoir barely supports the overlying column, so that threshold is near zero and the AOR is very large; under the measured subhydrostatic case it shrinks dramatically. This swing is why a Phase-1 pressure test gates the whole AOR.
ΔP(r,t) = (Q · μw) / (4π · k · heff) × W(u) where W(u) is the Theis well function, u = r² · φ · μw · ct / (4 · k · t)
Technical Classical Theis (1935) for confined aquifer transient pressure. Solution in terms of the exponential integral W(u) — approximated here with Cooper-Jacob (u<0.01) and series expansion (0.01<u<1). Bisection solver for r at ΔP_crit. Table on tab sweeps r from 100 m to 100 km and shows how quickly ΔP drops off.

Tab 12 · DFIT

Plain English DFIT = "Diagnostic Fracture Injection Test". A short test you run before real injection where you pump a tiny amount of water at high rate, then shut in and watch how pressure decays. The decay pattern tells you the horizontal stress in the rock and the fracture propagation pressure. From that you compute the maximum BHP you're allowed to run at. Required by AER before permitting.
Gap: σhmin ≈ 17.3 MPa at 1050 m (16.5 kPa/m × 1050 m) is a regional placeholder from Lund Snee & Zoback (2020). A dedicated DFIT on the first injector is needed before FID. If real DFIT shows σhmin is lower than ~17 MPa, the injection-rate ceiling drops proportionally — and at this shallow depth the ΔP window is already tight (~4 MPa).

Required DFIT deliverables for the regulator

  1. ISIP (Instantaneous Shut-In Pressure)
  2. Fracture closure pressure (G-function analysis)
  3. Pore pressure (radial flow signature)
  4. Leakoff coefficient
  5. Fracture gradient
  6. σ_hmin estimate (closure is preferred; ISIP is acceptable)
  7. Poisson's ratio back-calc vs log-based σ_hmin
  8. Biot α (from pulse-decay or independent measurement)

Tab 13 · MICP

Plain English MICP = "Mercury Injection Capillary Pressure". Test where you squeeze mercury (a non-wetting fluid — like CO₂) into a rock sample and measure the pressure needed to get it past the tiny seal rock pore throats. High pressure needed = tight seal = good caprock. We translate the lab mercury value into an equivalent CO₂/brine value, then calculate how tall a column of CO₂ the caprock can hold before it leaks.
Max column height = Pentry,CO₂/brine / [(ρw − ρCO₂) · g]

Our Nisku caprock (Calmar Fm / Ireton shale) holds ~180 m of CO₂ column vs 65 m reservoir thickness → 2.8× safety factor.

Gap: Pentry = 12.5 MPa is a regional shale analog (Calmar Fm). Need dedicated MICP on 15-32 caprock cuttings to confirm.

Tab 14 · SCAL

Plain English SCAL = "Special Core Analysis". Lab tests that measure how CO₂ and brine flow past each other when both are in the rock at the same time. Result is "relative permeability curves" — the endpoints (krg* = 0.45, krw* = 0.35) and shape of how permeability drops as saturation changes. These curves feed the injectivity and plume calculations.
Technical Brooks-Corey / Corey rel-perm. Drainage (CO₂ displaces brine). Parameters: Swi = 0.22, Sgr = 0.28, krg* = 0.45, krw* = 0.35, ng = 2, nw = 2.5. Default values drawn from SCAL analogs in Nisku special core data Accumap folder — 21 regional SCAL PDFs.

Tab 15 · XRD_Mineralogy

Plain English XRD = "X-Ray Diffraction". A lab test that fires X-rays at powdered rock and reads back what minerals are in it by percentage. Physics primer · Capacity (Bachu volumetric)

Plain English Think of the rock as a big sponge. You're trying to figure out how much CO₂ soda you can squirt into it before it overflows. The sponge's total volume × how holey it is × how much of the holes are accessible × how dense the soda is = how many kilograms of soda fit.
Technical

E is the fudge factor that captures everything the simple volumetrics can't: heterogeneity, gravity override, mobility contrast, pressure compartmentalization. Bachu (2008/2015) gives 1–4% for open saline aquifers without pressure management.

Physics primer · Injectivity (Mathias radial)

Plain English Pushing CO₂ into rock is like pushing water through a coffee filter — you need some extra pressure above the ambient. How much extra pressure depends on: how fast you're pushing (Q), how sticky the CO₂ is (μ), how holey the rock is (k), how thick it is (h), and how far from the well you need to drive flow (ln of radius).
Technical
ΔPbh = (Qres · μCO₂) / (2π · k · krg · hperf) · [ln(re/rw) + s]

Classical radial steady-state Darcy. Mathias (2009) adapts it for CO₂ with relative-permeability and buoyancy corrections; for a Phase-1 estimate the krg·h formulation above is sufficient.

Units check: Q in m³/s, μ in Pa·s, k in m² (multiply mD × 9.869e-16), h in m → ΔP in Pa.

Our result (1 Mt/yr through one well, stress test): Q_res = 0.044 m³/s, μ = 5e-5, k = 2.12e-13 m², krg = 0.45, h = 37 m, ln(500/0.11) = 8.4 → ΔP ≈ 1.0 MPa. BHP = P_init + ΔP = 12.3 MPa vs ceiling 15.6 MPa (0.9 × 17.3). OK at brine-hydrostatic, with ~3 MPa to spare — tighter than a deep reservoir.

Physics primer · Plume (Nordbotten-Celia similarity)

Plain English After 25 years of injecting, how wide is the "footprint" of CO₂ in the reservoir? Take the total volume injected, divide by the accessible pore space per square meter, that gives you area → radius. But CO₂ is slipperier than brine, so it fingers ahead of the volume balance. Correct with √M.
Technical
rplume(t) = √[Vres(t) / (π · h · φ · (1−Swi))] · √M

Nordbotten & Celia (2006) derived this from the coupled momentum-mass balance under sharp-interface assumptions. The √M factor accounts for the mobility contrast — the leading edge of the plume advances faster than piston displacement.

M = (μ_w · krw*) / (μ_CO₂ · krg*) — the "endpoint mobility ratio". Our M ≈ 8, √M ≈ 2.8. That means the plume radius is 2.8× the volume-balance radius — a big correction.

Physics primer · Pressure front (Theis)

Plain English When you start injecting, pressure at the well goes up immediately. That pressure increase spreads outward like ripples in a pond — but the pond is underground, and the "ripple" dampens fast because the rock absorbs pressure (compressibility). Theis (1935) gives the exact shape of the ripple. At some radius the pressure increase drops below whatever value we've chosen as "enough to worry about" — that's our AOR radius.
Technical
ΔP(r,t) = (Q · μw) / (4π · k · heff) · W(u) u = r² · φ · μw · ct / (4 · k · t)

W(u) is the Theis well function, also known as the exponential integral −Ei(−u). It's infinite at r=0, falls off logarithmically for small u, and exponentially for large u. Numerical approximations:

  • u < 0.01: Cooper-Jacob: W ≈ −0.5772 − ln(u)
  • 0.01 < u < 1: series: W ≈ −0.5772 − ln(u) + u − u²/4 + u³/18 …
  • u > 1: asymptotic: W ≈ e^(−u)/u · (1 − 1/u + 2/u² − …)

Regulatory reality. EPA Class VI uses ΔP_crit = the pressure increase that would allow brine to rise through the worst legacy well to a depth where it contaminates underground sources of drinking water. For most basins that works out to 1–2 MPa. Our 1.5 MPa is the middle default.

Physics primer · Seal capacity (MICP)

Plain English The caprock (Calmar shale) is a lid. The tighter the pores in that lid, the more pressure you need before CO₂ squeezes through. Also, the denser the CO₂ is compared to the brine it's displacing, the less buoyancy force there is pushing the CO₂ up through the lid. Both effects combine to give a "maximum column height" of CO₂ the lid can hold. Ours is about 180 m — way more than our 65 m reservoir needs.
Technical
hmax = Pentry,CO₂/brine / [(ρw − ρCO₂) · g]

Lab MICP uses Hg/air (high IFT, 480 mN/m). In-situ is CO₂/brine (lower IFT ~30 mN/m at supercritical, contact angle ~30°). Scale lab Pentry by (IFTCO₂/brine · cos θ) / IFTHg/air ≈ 0.054 factor. A 12.5 MPa lab entry → 0.68 MPa in-situ CO₂/brine entry pressure → ~180 m holdback column against the ~385 kg/m³ density contrast (brine-hydrostatic; under subhydrostatic the contrast rises to ~760 kg/m³ and the column nearly halves).

Glossary · A — F

AER Directive 065
Alberta Energy Regulator's rulebook for fluid injection and disposal wells. Specifies testing, monitoring, and BHP ceilings. Technical: mandatory compliance framework for any Alberta subsurface injection permit.
AOI (Area of Interest)
The broader area you're studying — usually bigger than the lease. Includes neighbors' wells, regional geology, etc.
AOR (Area of Review)
The radius around your injector where existing wells have to be reviewed for integrity. Defined by pressure increase, not CO₂ plume. Technical: in EPA Class VI, the AOR is the region within which ΔP exceeds a critical value that could force brine up a legacy well to contaminate USDWs.
Bachu (Stefan Bachu)
The godfather of CCS capacity assessment. Former Alberta Geological Survey; author of the 1–4% efficiency factor you'll see everywhere.
BHP (Bottomhole Pressure)
The pressure at the bottom of the injection well, right at the reservoir. This is what matters for fracturing — not surface pressure. Technical: BHP = P_init + ΔP_bh at injection steady state. Compared against 0.9·σ_hmin ceiling.
Biot α
Poroelastic coupling coefficient. How much of the pore pressure translates into effective stress change. Dimensionless, 0–1. Our default: 0.75. Technical: σ' = σ − α·Pp (Terzaghi-Biot).
Calmar Formation
The shale that sits on top of the Nisku. Our caprock. The reason CO₂ stays down there.
Caprock
The impermeable layer above the reservoir that seals the CO₂ in. Without it, no storage project is possible.
CBL (Cement Bond Log)
A logging tool run inside a well to evaluate how well the cement bonded to the casing and the formation. Bad bonding = potential leak path.
CCS
Carbon Capture and Storage. (Sometimes CCUS: Carbon Capture, Utilization and Storage.)
Class VI well
The US EPA designation for wells dedicated to CO₂ sequestration. Governed by 40 CFR 146.84. Stricter than Class II (disposal).
Corey / Brooks-Corey
Two-parameter curves that describe how permeability to each fluid changes with saturation. You give the endpoint and the exponent, you get a curve.
CSA Z741
Canadian Standards Association standard for geological storage of CO₂. Sets the Canadian rules for CCS site qualification.
DFIT (Diagnostic Fracture Injection Test)
A short, high-rate injection test used to measure σ_hmin, frac closure pressure, and pore pressure. Required before CCS injection permits in Alberta.
DST (Drill-Stem Test)
A well test run during drilling to measure reservoir pressure and flow capacity. Older tech, but gives you decades of historical data.
Dunham classification
The standard way to name carbonate rocks by texture: mudstone, wackestone, packstone, grainstone, boundstone, crystalline. Higher-energy = coarser = usually more permeable.
E (Efficiency factor)
The fraction of the theoretical pore volume that can actually store CO₂. Accounts for heterogeneity, buoyancy override, mobility contrast, pressure buildup. 1-4% default (Bachu).
EOS (Equation of State)
A thermodynamic model relating pressure, volume, temperature, and composition. For CO₂ the reference is Span & Wagner (1996).
FD (Full-Diameter) core
Core plug cut at full well-bore diameter (~4 inch), rather than the standard 1-inch small plug. Better for heterogeneous carbonates where small plugs can miss pore features.
FID (Final Investment Decision)
The go/no-go moment on a project. After FID, capital is committed.
Fracture gradient (FG)
The pressure per unit depth required to propagate a hydraulic fracture. Typically 14–20 kPa/m in the WCSB. Our Nisku: 16.5 kPa/m.

Glossary · G — P

Geomean (geometric mean)
The preferred average for log-normally distributed variables like permeability. Take the log of everything, average, then exponentiate back. For our 45 samples, geomean Kmax = 215 mD vs arithmetic mean which would be inflated by the handful of >1000 mD outliers.
He-porosity
Helium porosity — the standard measurement. Helium atoms are small enough to enter essentially all pores, giving you total connected porosity.
HPC (High Performance Computing)
Big compute clusters needed for full-physics reservoir simulation. Not used in this Phase-1 screening.
IFT (Interfacial Tension)
The surface tension between two immiscible fluids. CO₂/brine IFT ~30 mN/m at supercritical conditions. Determines capillary entry pressure.
Injectivity index
Rate per unit pressure drop (Q/ΔP). The simple one-number metric of "how well does this well take fluid?".
ISIP (Instantaneous Shut-In Pressure)
The pressure immediately after you stop pumping during a DFIT. Upper bound on σ_hmin. Key DFIT output.
JIP (Joint Industry Project)
Multi-operator collaborative R&D. SLB-JIP and WASP are both JIPs. Pools cost and data across operators.
Kmax, K90, Kv
Permeability measured in three directions: maximum horizontal (Kmax), 90° to it (K90), and vertical (Kv). Ratio Kv/Kmax tells you layering. <0.1 = strongly layered.
krg, krw (relative permeabilities)
How easily CO₂ flows through rock partially saturated with brine (krg), and vice versa (krw). Endpoint values krg* and krw* are what matters for most calculations.
m² vs mD (permeability units)
Rock permeability. 1 mD = 9.869e-16 m². Petroleum engineers use mD (values 0.01–10,000 legible); physics uses m².
Mathias (Simon Mathias)
UK researcher. Co-authored the closed-form CCS injectivity solution (2009) that we use — an extension of classical Darcy radial flow.
MICP (Mercury Injection Capillary Pressure)
A lab test that pushes mercury into a rock sample under increasing pressure, measuring the pressure needed to enter successively smaller pore throats. Gives us seal capacity.
MIT (Mechanical Integrity Test)
Annual pressure test required on injection wells to prove casing integrity. ≤10% pressure drop over 30 min = pass.
NTG (Net-to-Gross)
The fraction of total rock thickness that is actually flowable reservoir. A 65 m Nisku with NTG = 0.65 has ~42 m of "net pay". Reduces the 65 m gross in all volumetric calculations.
Nisku Formation
Upper Devonian (Frasnian) carbonate ramp in western Canada. Our target reservoir. Complex facies: reef buildups, open marine shelf, basin-margin shales. Pine/Oak/Cedar injection zone: ~65 m thick at 1010–1090 m depth (the 15-32 analog core is deeper, at 1863–1920 m).
Nordbotten & Celia
Princeton researchers who produced the similarity solution for CO₂ plume radius (2006 JFM paper). Our plume tab uses their equation.
P10 / P50 / P90
Probabilistic exceedance values. P10 = 10% probability value is at least this. P50 = median. P90 = 90% probability. Oilfield convention: P10 is PESSIMISTIC (10% chance of being even lower). This reverses from some statistics traditions.
Plume
The physical volume of injected CO₂ in the reservoir. NOT the same as the pressure footprint (AOR). Always smaller than AOR.
Pore throat
The narrowest constriction between two pores. Fluid has to squeeze through these. Pore throat distribution (from MICP) is what controls permeability and seal capacity.
Pressure gradient
Pressure per unit depth (kPa/m). Virgin hydrostatic ~10 kPa/m. Our regional Nisku: 7.5 kPa/m (underpressured — decades of production have depleted it).

Glossary · Q — Z

Q (flow rate)
In CCS, usually given as Mt/yr at surface (Q_surf) or m³/s at reservoir conditions (Q_res = Q_surf / ρ_CO₂).
Relative permeability (krg, krw)
See "kr" entry. Two-phase flow modifier on absolute permeability.
Reservoir
Any permeable rock that can store fluids. For CCS specifically: porous rock below a sealing caprock at sufficient depth (>~800 m) that CO₂ is supercritical.
Residual trapping (Sgr)
After injection stops and brine moves back in, some CO₂ is permanently stranded in pore corners as disconnected bubbles. Sgr = 0.28 means 28% of pore volume traps CO₂ this way. Long-term storage security.
Rock fabric (RF)
A pore-network classification scheme. We used RF1 (excellent, K>500 mD), RF2 (good), RF3 (moderate), RF4 (tight). Drives flow-unit layering in simulation.
SCAL (Special Core Analysis)
Lab tests beyond routine core analysis. Relative permeability, capillary pressure, electrical properties, NMR. Expensive ($50-200k per sample) but critical for CCS.
Skin (s)
Dimensionless number representing additional pressure drop at the wellbore due to formation damage (positive skin) or stimulation (negative skin). 0 = undamaged. +5 to +10 = damaged.
Span-Wagner (1996)
The reference equation of state for CO₂. Accurate to <0.1% across supercritical conditions. NIST REFPROP implements it.
Storativity (S)
In groundwater hydrology, the volume of water released per unit area per unit pressure drop. Controls how pressure spreads. Dimensionless.
Supercritical CO₂
Above 31°C and 7.4 MPa. Behaves as a single phase — denser than gas, less viscous than liquid. This is the state CO₂ is in at reservoir conditions > ~800 m deep.
Swi (irreducible water saturation)
The minimum water saturation after CO₂ drainage. Water that's so tightly held by capillary forces it can't be displaced. Our Nisku default: 0.22.
σ_hmin (sigma-h-min)
Minimum horizontal stress. The pressure threshold above which hydraulic fracturing occurs. THE key geomechanical number for CCS. Measured by DFIT.
Theis (1935)
Classical transient pressure diffusion solution in a confined aquifer. The foundation of AOR analysis.
TVDSS (True Vertical Depth Sub-Sea)
Elevation measured from sea level downward. Pine/Oak/Cedar Nisku top: ~1010 m TVDSS (15-32 analog core top: 1863 m).
USDW (Underground Source of Drinking Water)
In EPA Class VI, aquifers with TDS < 10,000 mg/L. AOR exists to protect these.
UWI (Unique Well Identifier)
Canadian well numbering scheme: 100/LSD-SEC-TWP-RGE-MER. Example: 100/15-32-050-03W5/00 = LSD 15, Section 32, Township 50, Range 3, West of 5th Meridian.
Viscosity (μ)
Resistance to flow. Water ~1 cP. Brine at reservoir T: 0.6 cP. Supercritical CO₂: ~0.06 cP (10× less than water). Drives mobility contrast.
WASP
Wabamun Area Sequestration Project. Multi-operator regional CCS study ~2010-2012. The grandfather of our analysis.
WCSB
Western Canada Sedimentary Basin. The ~1.4 million km² sedimentary basin covering most of Alberta, parts of BC and Saskatchewan.
XRD (X-Ray Diffraction)
Lab technique that identifies mineral phases by the crystal lattice diffraction pattern. Gives you % composition of each mineral in a rock sample.
