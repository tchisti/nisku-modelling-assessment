📘 Step-by-Step Walkthrough for the Team

Read this top to bottom. Everything the model does, explained in plain engineering language. Use this to defend assumptions in a review meeting.

1 What we measured vs what we assumed

The model uses three data tiers, colour-coded throughout:

  • DATA — measured on our own lease: 45 full-diameter core plugs at 15-32, 20 at 8-17, Schlumberger XRD, TRA. These are hard numbers, not estimates.
  • LIT — from the public literature or Bachu/Stoakes regional studies. Used for temperature gradient, frac gradient, CO₂ PVT.
  • ASSUMP — engineering judgement where no measurement exists (e.g. skin = 0 at start, storage efficiency E = 3%).

Every output is a function of these three tiers. Change a slider in the sidebar and the chain recomputes.

2 The reservoir picture we're defending

Nisku at 15-32 is a 57-m gross dolomitic ramp carbonate sandwiched between the Ireton/Calmar shales above (the seal) and the Cooking Lake platform below. Porosity averages 8% with a "sweet spot" reaching 17.7% in grainstones. Permeability geometric mean is 215 mD — that's a good saline-aquifer target; for reference, Sleipner sand is ~2,000 mD, Quest is 100–500 mD. Vertical permeability is 3% of horizontal, meaning CO₂ can't easily buoy up through the reservoir column — it stays trenched in the thief zones. That's a containment positive but also a caprock-stress reminder: the lateral plume gets bigger.

3 How we compute storage capacity

We use the CSA Z741 / Bachu volumetric equation:

MCO₂ = A · h · φ · (N/G) · (1 − Swi) · ρCO₂ · E

Every factor: A is lease area, h gross thickness, φ porosity, N/G net-to-gross, (1−Swi) mobile-brine fraction that CO₂ can displace, ρCO₂ in-situ CO₂ density (720 kg/m³ at reservoir P/T), E is storage efficiency — the fraction of pore volume that actually sees CO₂ in a realistic, gravity-stratified plume. For saline aquifers, Bachu recommends 1–4%. We use 3%. This is the single biggest lever in the equation. If you want to push back on capacity numbers, stress-test E first.

Monte Carlo runs 10,000 realizations varying φ (p10/p50/p90 from core), E (uniform 1–5%), h, N/G. Output is a distribution — we report P10 / P50 / P90 capacity.

4 How we compute injectivity

We solve the radial-flow Darcy equation with CO₂ as the flowing phase (gas relative perm, not absolute perm):

ΔPwf = (Qres · μCO₂) / (2π · k · krg · hperf) · [ln(re/rw) + S]

Qres is rate in reservoir m³/s (surface Mt/yr ÷ ρCO₂), μCO₂ is 0.06 cP, krg end-point is 0.45. Skin S starts at 0 (greenfield) but can be bumped to capture near-well damage, salt precipitation, or hydrate risk.

Hard limit: BHP ≤ 0.9 × σhmin per AER Directive 065. Our frac pressure at 1890 m × 16.5 kPa/m = 31.2 MPa → BHPmax = 28.1 MPa. Initial P = 19.8 MPa → we have a 8.3-MPa injection window. The model flags red if computed ΔP exceeds this.

5 How we compute the CO₂ plume

The Nordbotten–Celia 2006 similarity solution treats the plume as a gravity tongue in a confined aquifer. At late time, plume radius scales as:

rCO₂(t) ≈ √( Vinj(t) / (π·h·φ·(1−Swi)) )

Vinj(t) is cumulative reservoir volume injected. This is the upper-bound lateral footprint. We then add a mobility-ratio correction (M ≈ 10 unfavourable for CO₂ displacing brine) that inflates the radius by ~√M. The plume plan view is drawn as a circle — the cross-section shows the characteristic gravity-override tongue where CO₂ occupies a thin slice at the top of the reservoir and brine the rest. With Kv/Kh = 0.03 the tongue is thinner than in a homogeneous case — good for capacity per area, bad for caprock stress distribution.

6 How we delineate AOR

AOR is the bigger of (a) the final stabilized CO₂ plume footprint, and (b) the pressure-front radius where ΔP exceeds the critical value that could drive brine up through a legacy well into a USDW (Underground Source of Drinking Water). We use the Theis line-source solution:

ΔP(r,t) = (Q·μw)/(4π·k·h) · W(u),   u = (r²·S) / (4·k·h·t/μw)

where W(u) is the Theis well function. We sweep r until ΔP = ΔPcrit (default 1.5 MPa — a typical value; refine with actual brine-column calculation once we know USDW base depth). Any legacy well inside that radius is in the AOR and must be assessed for annular cement integrity. The disposal-well inventory in the Larger AOI workbook is overlaid on the map.

7 How DFIT, MICP, SCAL, XRD bolt in

DFIT gives us σhmin and therefore the 0.9× fracture-gradient BHP ceiling. Without DFIT we're estimating from regional gradients — you want to do a 15-32 injection test before committing capital.

MICP (mercury intrusion) on caprock gives capillary entry pressure → maximum CO₂ column the seal can hold before breakthrough. We default to 1.8 MPa (Calmar shale analog); real 15-32 caprock MICP would be better. Column height > planned gas column = seal OK.

SCAL (special core analysis) gives relative permeability curves (Brooks-Corey fit here) and residual trapping fraction. These feed the plume and injectivity calcs. Residual trap of ~30% means that during post-injection, 30% of the swept volume stays as immobile CO₂ — a durable trapping mechanism on top of dissolution and mineral.

XRD tells us it's dolomite + minor anhydrite. Implication: slow CO₂-mineral reaction (dolomite is sluggish with carbonic acid), so most secure trapping over 100 years is structural + residual + dissolution; mineral trapping is a long-term bonus, not a short-term plan.

8 How to defend the numbers in a review

  1. Lead with the headline: P50 capacity is [X] Mt. We can hold 25 years of 1 Mt/yr injection inside the lease.
  2. Open the Reservoir tab — show the 45-point φ-K cloud. It's real, measured, on our core. This is stronger than any modeled number.
  3. Flip to Injectivity. Show the ΔP window. That's where the conversation will go to — is the rate physically achievable without fracturing the caprock? Answer: yes, with margin.
  4. Flip to AOR. Show the polygon overlaid on legacy disposal wells. This is the regulator's first question.
  5. Close with the risk table: caprock seal integrity (need 15-32 MICP), legacy-well remediation (3–5 wells inside AOR), and induced-seismicity monitoring (none forecast but MMV plan required).

9 What this model does NOT do

  • It does not run full 3D multi-phase simulation (CMG-GEM / TOUGH2). Analytical solutions are upper-bound conservative but miss heterogeneity and coupled chemistry.
  • It does not include geomechanical coupling — fault reactivation risk should be assessed separately with a Mohr-Coulomb screening on regional 3D seismic.
  • It does not model salt precipitation ("halite scaling") near wellbore, which in 210 kppm brine can be a real injectivity killer in the first 100 days. Use skin sensitivity to bracket it.
  • It does not simulate pressure maintenance via brine production (reverse-osmosis disposal) — that's a Phase 2 option if AOR becomes too big.

10 Next 5 engineering decisions

  1. Run a DFIT at 15-32 to confirm σhmin — this is the single biggest uncertainty killer.
  2. MICP on Calmar shale caprock cuttings/core — defend the seal before the regulator does.
  3. SCAL drainage/imbibition on 3 plugs at 1866, 1885, 1920 m — one per flow unit.
  4. Build the static geological model in Petrel, upscale to a CMG-GEM simulation grid at 10 m × 10 m × 1 m near-well, 100 m × 100 m × 10 m far-field.
  5. Inventory all wells penetrating the Nisku inside AOR polygon, pull cement bond logs, build a remediation plan with costs and timing.
